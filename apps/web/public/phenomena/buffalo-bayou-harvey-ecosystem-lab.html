What happened to Buffalo Bayou?

In August 2017, Hurricane Harvey dropped 51 inches of rain on Houston in 5 days. Streets flooded. Homes were destroyed. But something invisible happened too.

Water rushing through Houston's streets picked up pollutants, nutrients from fertilizers, and raw sewage — and poured it all directly into Buffalo Bayou.

Over the next steps, you'll see how that changed the ecosystem — and why it still hasn't fully recovered.

Buffalo Bayou Before Harvey

Before Harvey, Buffalo Bayou was stressed but functional. Houston's concrete landscape meant runoff was already high — but dissolved oxygen was still acceptable for most aquatic life.

The bayou supported 18 fish species, riparian plants, and a connected food web linking insects, birds, turtles, and mammals.

Harvey Hits Houston

51 inches on 60% concrete — with nowhere to go. Runoff surged into Buffalo Bayou carrying a toxic mix.

Day 1–2: The Flood
Floodwaters overwhelmed storm drains and mixed with raw sewage from flooded treatment plants. Turbidity spiked from 38 → 890 NTU. Sunlight couldn't reach underwater plants.
Day 3–4: Nutrient Surge
Floodwaters washed fertilizers from lawns and farms into the bayou. Nitrogen jumped 2.1 → 8.7 mg/L. This fueled a massive algae bloom.
Day 5+: Oxygen Crash
As the bloom died, decomposers consumed nearly all dissolved oxygen. Hypoxia set in — O₂ crashed to 2.1 mg/L. Fish died.

The Aftermath

Within days, Buffalo Bayou's biodiversity collapsed. Changes to abiotic factors triggered a chain reaction through the entire food web.
