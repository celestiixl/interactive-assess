How Do Plants Keep Themselves Alive?

Transport · Reproduction · Response — and how their structures make it all possible

🌿 Phenomenon
🔬 Simulation

Every spring, Texas roadsides explode with bluebonnets — but only for a few weeks.

Millions of bluebonnets bloom along Texas highways every March and April, then seem to vanish. The seeds can survive in soil for decades, waiting. When conditions are just right — the right amount of winter cold, spring rain, and warm temperatures — they all seem to bloom at the same time. No brain. No nervous system. Just structure doing its job.

🤔 Driving Question

A bluebonnet seed waits 50 years underground. When conditions are right, it germinates, moves water 12 inches up a stem using xylem, sends sugar to growing flowers using phloem, bends toward sunlight, attracts a bee to pollinate it, and produces seeds that spread across the highway. How does a plant without a brain pull all of this off — and how do its structures make each step possible?

Three Systems That Work Together

B.12B asks you to explain how these three systems interact — not just define them separately. The bluebonnet uses all three to survive. Changing one affects the others.

💧

Transport

Xylem moves water and minerals up from the roots — pulled by transpiration from the leaves. Phloem moves sugar in all directions from where it's made to where it's needed.

Reproduction

Sexual: Flowers attract pollinators → pollen meets egg → fertilization → seed. Asexual: Runners, rhizomes, or fragmentation produce clones without fertilization.

Response (Tropisms)

Plants grow toward or away from stimuli using hormones. Auxin makes stems bend toward light (phototropism). Roots always grow downward (gravitropism). ABA causes stomata to close during drought.

tropism auxin gibberellins
💡 The big idea: In B.12B, structure enables function. Xylem tubes are hollow and dead — perfect for water flow. Phloem cells stay alive — they have to actively load and unload sugar. The shape of a flower attracts specific pollinators. Every structure you study has a reason it's built that way.

💧 Xylem — Water Highway

Xylem cells are hollow and dead — like a pipe. Water enters through root hairs, travels up through xylem tubes, and exits through tiny pores in leaves called stomata. This one-way upward pull is called transpiration.

Soil water → Roots →
Xylem → Leaves → Air

🍬 Phloem — Sugar Highway

Phloem cells are alive and actively move sugar. Leaves make sugar through photosynthesis, then load it into phloem. The sugar flows to roots, fruits, or growing parts that can't photosynthesize. This movement is called translocation.

Leaves (make sugar) →
Phloem → Roots/Fruit

Gibberellins

Stimulates stem elongation and seed germination. Helps seeds "wake up" after dormancy — like Texas wildflower seeds after rain.

Ethylene

A gas hormone. Triggers fruit ripening, leaf drop, and response to injury. Produced when plants are stressed or aging.

ABA (Abscisic Acid)

The "stress hormone." Causes stomata to close during drought to prevent water loss. Keeps seeds dormant until conditions improve.

💡 Sexual vs. Asexual: Sexual reproduction (pollination → fertilization) creates offspring with new genetic combinations — more variation, better adaptation over time. Asexual reproduction creates identical clones — faster, but no genetic variety. Both strategies help plants survive in different environments.

Unit 7 Vocabulary

Tap any word to see its definition. Vocabulary is organized by tier — knowing which tier a word belongs to helps you use it correctly in writing and on the test.

🟢 Tier 1 — Everyday Words

Words you already know from daily life that show up in science too.

Root The underground part of a plant that absorbs water and minerals from the soil and anchors the plant in place.
Stem The main stalk of a plant that supports leaves, flowers, and transports materials between roots and leaves.
Leaf Flat, thin plant organ where most photosynthesis happens. Contains chlorophyll and stomata.
Seed A structure that contains a plant embryo and stored food, protected by a seed coat. Produced after fertilization.
Flower The reproductive structure of flowering plants (angiosperms). Contains the parts needed for pollination and fertilization.
Growth An increase in the size or number of cells in an organism. In plants, growth occurs at meristems — zones of actively dividing cells.

🟠 Tier 2 — Academic Words

Words used across many school subjects — important for reading and writing in science.

Transport The movement of materials from one place to another. In plants, the transport system moves water and sugar through the entire plant.
Reproduction The process by which organisms make new offspring. Can be sexual (involving fertilization) or asexual (no fertilization needed).
Response A reaction to a stimulus (something in the environment). Plants respond to light, gravity, touch, and chemical signals.
System A group of parts that work together to perform a function. The transport, reproduction, and response systems in plants all interact.
Structure The way something is built or organized. In B.12B, structure enables function — a hollow xylem tube is structured perfectly for water flow.
Function The job or purpose of a structure. Example: the function of xylem is to transport water upward through the plant.

🔵 Tier 3 — Biology Content Words

Words specific to biology. These are the most important for STAAR and will appear on tests.

Xylem Vascular tissue made of hollow, dead cells that transports water and dissolved minerals upward from roots to leaves. Driven by transpiration.
Phloem Vascular tissue made of living cells that transports sugar (made by photosynthesis) throughout the plant — up, down, or sideways — to wherever energy is needed.
Transpiration The process of water evaporating from leaves through stomata. This "pulls" water up through xylem — like sucking through a straw. It's what drives xylem flow.
Translocation The movement of sugar through phloem. From source (where sugar is made — usually leaves) to sink (where it's used or stored — roots, fruit, seeds).
Stomata Tiny pores (openings) on leaves that allow gas exchange and water vapor to escape. Surrounded by guard cells that open and close the pore. Plural of stoma.
Tropism A plant's directional growth in response to a stimulus. Positive = grows toward stimulus. Negative = grows away. Types: phototropism (light), gravitropism (gravity), thigmotropism (touch).
Phototropism A plant's growth toward (positive) or away from (negative) light. Stems show positive phototropism. Caused by uneven distribution of the hormone auxin.
Gravitropism Growth in response to gravity. Roots show positive gravitropism (grow down). Stems show negative gravitropism (grow up). Helps plants orient themselves.
Auxin A plant hormone produced at stem tips that causes cells to elongate (grow longer). Builds up on the shaded side of stems, causing the plant to bend toward light.
Gibberellins Plant hormones that stimulate stem elongation, seed germination, and fruit development. Gibberellins tell dormant seeds to start growing when conditions are right.
Ethylene A gas hormone that triggers fruit ripening, leaf drop (abscission), and responses to injury. A plant releasing ethylene signals nearby fruits to ripen at the same time.
ABA (Abscisic Acid) The "stress hormone." ABA causes stomata to close when the plant is losing too much water. It also keeps seeds dormant until conditions are safe for germination.
Pollination The transfer of pollen from the anther (male part) to the stigma (female part) of a flower. Can happen by wind, water, insects, birds, or bats. Must happen before fertilization.
Fertilization The fusion of a sperm cell (from pollen) and an egg cell inside the flower's ovule. Produces a zygote that develops into a seed. Fertilization ≠ pollination.
Seed Dispersal The movement of seeds away from the parent plant. Methods: wind (dandelion), animals (burrs), water (coconuts), explosion (squirting cucumber). Reduces competition with parent.
Vascular Tissue The internal "plumbing" of a plant — includes xylem and phloem bundled together. Found in roots, stems, and leaves as vascular bundles.
